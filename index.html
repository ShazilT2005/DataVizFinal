Where it Pays to Attend College

By Shazil Taqi

This project examines the relationship between college selection and graduates' average salaries by analyzing a broad range of data, including alumni surveys and employment records, while challenging the belief that prestigious colleges always lead to higher earnings. Through interactive visualizations, such as charts and maps, we provide a detailed exploration of variables like college type and geographic location, offering a deeper understanding of how educational choices impact financial outcomes.

Parallel Coordinates Plot showing ranges of salary per major

The parallel coordinates plot allows for a comprehensive examination of salary distributions across various majors by displaying multiple variables simultaneously. Through this visualization, viewers can discern patterns and trends in salary progression over time, aiding in informed decision-making regarding career paths and educational pursuits.

Scatterplot of Median Salaries

This scatterplot visualizes the starting and mid-career median salaries by school type—like Ivy League, state colleges, and liberal arts institutions—highlighting disparities and trends in graduates' earnings. Interactive features allow users to hover for details and filter by factors like location or field of study, deepening the analysis of how school choice influences salary trajectories.

Strip Plot: Distribution of starting salaries in different types of colleges

Summary: The strip chart visualizes the distribution of starting salaries across different types of colleges. Each point on the chart represents a specific type of college, and the position of the point corresponds to the starting median salary for that type of college. The x-axis displays the types of colleges, while the y-axis represents the starting median salaries.

Bar Chart Matrix: Average salaries at different types of schools.

The provided code generates six bar charts, each representing different variables related to salary statistics for various types of schools. Here's a summary of each graph:

  1. Starting Median Salary: Average starting median salary for different types of schools, including Engineering, Party, and Liberal Arts schools.
  2. Mid-Career Median Salary: Average mid-career median salary for Engineering, Party, and Liberal Arts schools.
  3. Mid-Career 10th Percentile Salary: Average mid-career 10th percentile salary, indicating the lowest salary range among mid-career professionals for each school type.
  4. Mid-Career 25th Percentile Salary: Average mid-career 25th percentile salary, representing a slightly higher salary range compared to the 10th percentile.
  5. Mid-Career 75th Percentile Salary: Average mid-career 75th percentile salary, indicating a higher salary range, likely representing more successful professionals within each school type.
  6. Mid-Career 90th Percentile Salary: Average mid-career 90th percentile salary, representing the highest salary range among mid-career professionals for each school type, likely indicating the most successful individuals.

Conclusion

This project provides a detailed exploration of the relationship between college choice and graduate earnings, challenging the conventional wisdom that prestigious schools always lead to higher salaries. Through extensive data analysis and interactive visualizations, it is evident that the impact of educational choices on financial outcomes is multifaceted and influenced by a variety of factors including type of institution, field of study, and geographic location.

By utilizing dynamic tools like parallel coordinates plots, scatterplots, and bar charts, the project allows for an in-depth understanding of salary distributions and career progressions across different types of educational institutions. These insights empower students and families to make informed decisions, prioritizing personal goals and the specific benefits of each college over generalized prestige.

In conclusion, the findings underscore the importance of a nuanced approach to choosing an educational path that aligns with individual career aspirations and financial goals, moving beyond the traditional emphasis on institutional prestige to a more personalized consideration of educational value.
